The last radio station in the valley

By Amara Osei  ·  Published June 2024  ·  Community oral history

Story content

Listen to this story

Full transcript below Download transcript

About this story

WKLV was more than a radio station. For the 4,000 residents of Millfield Valley, it was the thread that held community together — morning calls, weather warnings, and voices that knew every name on every road.

"When the floods came, the only voice telling us to get out was WKLV. That's not something a streaming platform can do."

This oral history was recorded over three days in October 2023 with former station staff, longtime listeners, and the family of station founder Roy Briggs. The station broadcast for fifty-three years before going silent in March 2022.

Producer Amara Osei spoke with eleven community members across two counties. All interviews were conducted with informed consent and participants have reviewed their quoted material.

AMARA OSEI: The first thing everyone tells you about KVAL is the static. Not the bad kind — the living kind, the kind that meant the signal was fighting through mountain air to reach you.

[00:14]

DELLA RUIZ: My husband and I, we'd have it on every single morning. Six-thirty news, weather for the passes, farm report. We couldn't start the day without it.

[00:28]

AMARA OSEI: The station sat at four thousand feet elevation, in a prefabricated building that had originally been a ranger outpost. The transmitter was upgraded once, in 1981.

[00:42]

GLEN TAKAHASHI (former program director): We ran on a skeleton crew the whole time. Three full-time people in the good years. Toward the end it was two — myself and Roy's daughter Patrice.

[01:02]

AMARA OSEI: Roy Briggs built the station with his own hands in 1971. His daughter, Patrice, remembers watching him string the antenna cable across the roof of their family home.

[01:18]

PATRICE BRIGGS: He said the valley needed a voice. Not a big voice. Just a voice that knew it was home. He used to say that the mountains already had their own sound — he just wanted to give people a way to hear each other over them.

[02:00]

DELLA RUIZ: When the floods came in 2019 — that was when everything changed. The county emergency system went down. Cell towers were out. The only voice telling us to get out, telling us which roads were passable, was WKLV.

[02:18]

GLEN TAKAHASHI: We stayed on air for thirty-one hours straight. Patrice and I took turns sleeping on the floor. We had a generator, a phone line, and Roy's old address book of everyone in the valley who had a landline.

[02:35]

PATRICE BRIGGS: People called in from their porches as the water rose. We read out their names on air so their families would know they were okay. That's not something a streaming platform can do.

[03:00]

AMARA OSEI: The station closed fourteen months after the floods. Equipment failure, a collapse in local advertising, and the death of Roy Briggs in early 2021 proved too much to recover from. The transmitter went silent on a Tuesday morning in March 2022.

[03:20]

DELLA RUIZ: I turned on the radio like I always did and there was nothing. Just static. And I thought — well, I thought, there it is. The bad kind.

[03:58]

AMARA OSEI: The building still stands. Patrice Briggs visits it every year on her father's birthday. She says it still smells like coffee and solder and something she can only describe as frequency.

[04:14]

PATRICE BRIGGS: I want people to know it mattered. Not just during the flood — always. Every single morning that someone heard the weather and knew what to wear. Every night someone fell asleep to a voice that knew their town. That mattered. That was real.
